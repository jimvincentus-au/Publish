# Week 3 Appendix: Private Channels as Government

This was an acute rupture week: a sitting president and a private oligarch jointly pierced multiple guardrails at once. Executive power expanded through sweeping orders on tariffs, foreign aid, immigration, LGBT rights, DEI, and religion, while Musk’s DOGE operation gained de facto control over core state functions—Treasury payments, HR databases, USAID, Education, SBA, NOAA, CMS, and even air traffic control. The Justice Department and FBI were purged of those who investigated Trump and January 6, civil rights and police-reform enforcement was halted, and foreign-influence and kleptocracy units dismantled. Inspectors general were removed across agencies. Simultaneously, information systems were manipulated: CDC and climate data vanished, ICE rewrote its own history, and media access at the Pentagon and FCC was weaponized. Abroad, Trump launched a trade war, froze USAID, sanctioned the ICC, withdrew from UN human-rights bodies, and openly proposed a U.S. takeover of Gaza and harsher uses of Guantánamo. Courts, unions, states, and Congress mounted notable resistance—blocking the fund freeze, birthright-citizenship order, trans-prison transfers, and the federal buyout deadline—but these were defensive actions against a coordinated drive toward personalized, chaotic, and highly privatized rule.

Power and Authority

1. President Trump froze most foreign aid for a 90-day review and moved to fold USAID into the State Department (2025-02-01): By halting tens of billions in aid and curbing USAID’s independence, the administration concentrated foreign-assistance power in the executive, weakening a semi-autonomous development arm and its role in supporting democracy and stability abroad.

2. President Trump imposed sweeping tariffs on imports from Canada, Mexico, and China and then temporarily paused some of them (2025-02-01): Using emergency powers to levy and then partially delay broad tariffs on key partners let the executive unilaterally reshape trade conditions, injecting instability into the economy and foreign relations with limited legislative input.

3. 4. President Trump revoked a long-standing executive order on equal employment opportunity and contractor nondiscrimination (2025-02-01): Eliminating a 60‑year framework for equal opportunity in federal employment and contracting weakened formal safeguards against discrimination, especially for minorities and small businesses dependent on fair access to government work.

5. President Trump announced plans to end legal status programs for hundreds of thousands of migrants from several countries (2025-02-01): Targeting humanitarian parole and protection programs for migrants from Cuba, Haiti, Nicaragua, Venezuela, and Venezuela signaled an executive push to narrow who can safely reside in the U.S., heightening precarity for large diasporas.

6. 7. President Trump issued an executive order on combating antisemitism that encouraged immigration consequences for campus protesters (2025-02-02): Linking campus speech about Israel to potential deportation of foreign students blurred lines between protected expression and punishable conduct, inviting politicized use of immigration tools against dissenting viewpoints.

8.
