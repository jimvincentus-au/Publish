Title
-----
War as Routine Governance

Subtitle
--------
An unauthorized Iran war, weaponized immigration and voting rules, and curated secrecy deepen executive impunity while courts and civil society fight rearguard actions.

Tagline / Hook
-------------
No single rupture defined the week; instead, war, law, and information policy settled into a hardened pattern that makes reversal harder to imagine.

Synopses
--------
Long Synopsis:
Across Week 61, the Democracy Clock did not move, but the grooves of erosion deepened. Trump expanded the Iran war through unilateral bombardment and “no quarter” rhetoric, invoked emergency powers to reopen a spill‑prone pipeline, and claimed an absolute right to set tariffs and withdraw from NATO. Congress rejected a war‑powers limit, stumbled over DHS funding, and held no serious hearings on costs or strategy. Intelligence leaders trimmed their testimony to fit the White House line, while FBI purges, warrantless data purchases, and DHS AI projects widened surveillance. In immigration, expanded 287(g) policing, wrongful deportations, deaths in custody, and harsh charges against protesters turned enforcement into a laboratory for stratified citizenship. Simultaneously, the SAVE Act, state citizenship challenges, and DOJ voter‑roll suits narrowed access to the ballot under “integrity” branding. FCC license threats, curated Epstein disclosures, and politicized advisory bodies tightened control over information. Courts blocked some excesses, but mainly as embattled islands in a thickening web of unchecked authority.

Short Synopsis:
The clock stayed at 8:13 p.m. as war, emergency powers, immigration enforcement, and voting rules were woven into a denser architecture of unchecked executive authority and stratified citizenship, with courts offering limited resistance.

Actionable Outcomes
-------------------
- Support independent investigative journalism and local outlets that scrutinize war powers, immigration enforcement, and voting rules, especially those documenting planning failures and hidden costs.
- Back civil-rights and voting-rights organizations challenging documentary proof-of-citizenship laws, voter-roll purges, and citizenship-challenge rules in courts and statehouses.
- Engage with and, where possible, fund legal groups representing detainees, asylum seekers, and protesters facing terrorism or conspiracy charges, to reinforce due-process protections.
- Participate in or organize local oversight efforts—such as public records requests, town halls, and watchdog committees—focused on police use of force, 287(g) agreements, and detention conditions.
- Advocate to members of Congress and state legislators for stronger statutory limits on emergency powers, warrantless data purchases, and AI surveillance, including clear sunset clauses and transparency requirements.
- Use and promote credible fact-checking and media-literacy resources, including emerging AI tools evaluated by independent researchers, to counter coordinated disinformation about war and elections.
