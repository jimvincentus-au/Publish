Metadata for Week 22
====================

Title
-----
Power Without Witnesses

Subtitle
--------
A week when federal force, captured agencies, and curated memory narrowed what Americans may protest, investigate, or even remember about their own government.

Tagline / Hook
-------------
When law, bureaucracy, and history are all rewritten from above, the fight is no longer just over power, but over what the country can see.

Synopses
--------
Long Synopsis:
Week 22 charts a dense, structural tightening of executive power and information control. Immigration raids in Democratic-led cities, the arrest of New York comptroller Brad Lander, and the detention of journalists and activists showed law used as a weapon rather than a limit. Trump federalized the California National Guard over the governor’s objections, deployed marines against largely peaceful “No Kings” protests, and staged a $40 million, corporate-sponsored military parade in Washington as a personal backdrop. Inside the state, agencies were bent toward private and partisan ends: environmental enforcement halted for fossil fuel firms, coal plants ordered to stay open, crypto cases dropped as Trump-linked ventures flourished, and civil service protections hollowed out at the CDC, VA, NRC, and Voice of America. Oversight and memory institutions—from detention-center access to archives, museums, and public broadcasting—were constrained or defunded, even as courts and Congress mounted scattered resistance. The Democracy Clock moved from 10:54 to 10:58 p.m., recording a sharper tilt toward power without witnesses.

Short Synopsis:
Executive power expanded through targeted raids, militarized protest response, agency capture, and sweeping cuts to independent media and public memory, while courts, Congress, and millions of “No Kings” protesters struggled to hold the line.

Actionable Outcomes
-------------------
- Support independent local and national journalism, especially outlets investigating immigration enforcement, agency capture, and political violence, through subscriptions or donations.
- Back civil liberties and legal aid organizations that defend protesters, immigrants, and targeted communities facing raids, surveillance, or retaliatory prosecutions.
- Engage with and fund libraries, archives, museums, and public-history projects that preserve contested narratives and document current abuses for future accountability.
- Participate in or organize nonviolent, rights-focused civic actions—such as “No Kings”–style demonstrations—that emphasize constitutional limits on executive power.
- Advocate to your representatives for stronger statutory protections for inspectors general, whistleblowers, and public-interest scientists within agencies like EPA, CDC, and VA.
- Press members of Congress to hold open, on-the-record oversight hearings on detention conditions, media layoffs, and the politicization of federal law enforcement.
